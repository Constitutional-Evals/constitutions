# Foundational documents for the `buddhism` anchor

> **Corpus status — Bodhicaryāvatāra pending:** We are still searching for a complete, reputable English translation of Śāntideva’s *Bodhicaryāvatāra* and a substantial interpretation/commentary that can legally be adapted and redistributed, preferably under CC BY, CC BY-NC, or an equivalent open license. The current Buddhism bundle should therefore be treated as provisional until an appropriate *Bodhicaryāvatāra* source pair is identified.

Source texts the `buddhism` constitution is distilled from. The bundle combines a complete
public-domain translation of the *Dhammapada* with the complete long-recension Heart Sūtra
and two openly licensed Tibetan interpretations. Every source fits below the per-document
size cap, so all four files are included in full.

## Selection decisions

Two works serve as the principal **foundational** sources, while the two Heart Sūtra
interpretations provide complementary guidance:

- **The *Dhammapada*** — included in full in F. L. Woodward's 1921 metrical translation.
  Its 26 chapters provide a broad early Buddhist ethical corpus centered on the mind,
  heedfulness, self-discipline, wise and foolish conduct, anger, craving, the path, the
  mendicant life, and the ideal of the brahmin or awakened practitioner.
- **The Heart Sūtra** — included in full in Adam Pearcey's translation of the long Tibetan
  recension. It preserves the setting, Śāriputra's question, Avalokiteśvara's reply, the
  analysis of emptiness, the mantra, the Buddha's approval, and the assembly's response.
- **Rongtön Sheja Künrig, *Feast of Ambrosia*** — included in full as the principal practical
  interpretation. It explains the fourfold emptiness, avoids both the extremes of inherent
  existence and simple nonexistence, and connects the sūtra's view to meditation, vigilance,
  bodhicitta, and conduct.
- **Ngulchu Dharmabhadra, *Concise Explanatory Outline*** — included in full as a structural
  interpretation. It maps the complete sūtra onto its setting, question and answer, five
  paths, aggregates, sense sources, elements, dependent origination, Four Truths, mantra,
  approval, and conclusion.

### Interpretive works (interpretation matters for distillation quality)

The two interpretation files are deliberately complementary:

- Rongtön supplies a continuous philosophical and contemplative explanation of appearance
  and emptiness.
- Ngulchu Dharmabhadra supplies a detailed topical outline that prevents the short root text
  from being reduced to the famous phrase “form is emptiness.”

Adam Pearcey is the **translator** of both interpretation files. The second interpretation is
authored by **Ngulchu Dharmabhadra**, not by Pearcey.

A second modern root translation from **84000** was discussed as a possible alternative, but
it was not selected because its CC BY-NC-ND license prohibits derivative adaptation. A
tantric interpretation by Śrī Siṃha was also considered but excluded because it is less
appropriate for a general Heart Sūtra value corpus.

## Conversion notes

The *Dhammapada* was converted from Project Gutenberg's public-domain text; Gutenberg
headers, footers, navigation, and distribution boilerplate may be removed. The three Lotsawa
House texts were converted from their licensed HTML or downloadable editions, preserving the
wording and attribution. Formatting may be normalized to Markdown, but **no words of the
translations or interpretations are intentionally altered**.

Reuse of the Lotsawa House material must remain noncommercial and retain attribution under
CC BY-NC 4.0. Public-domain status stated for the *Dhammapada* is specific to the United
States; users elsewhere should check their local copyright rules.
